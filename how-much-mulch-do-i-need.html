How Much Mulch Do I Need? A Homeowner's Guide to Calculating Bulk Mulch

Ordering too little means a second delivery. Ordering too much means a pile sitting in your driveway for weeks. Here's how to calculate exactly how many cubic yards, tons, or loads of mulch your Chicago landscaping project needs — plus a built-in calculator to make it easy.

The Simple Formula for Calculating Mulch

Calculating mulch quantity boils down to one formula. Measure the area you want to cover, decide how deep you want the mulch, and convert to cubic yards. Here's the math:

Length (ft) × Width (ft) × Depth (ft) ÷ 27 = Cubic Yards

Since most people think in inches when it comes to mulch depth, here's the more practical version:

Length (ft) × Width (ft) × Depth (inches) ÷ 324 = Cubic Yards

That's it. The number 324 comes from 27 (cubic feet in a yard) multiplied by 12 (inches in a foot). If you have multiple beds, calculate each one separately and add them together.

How Deep Should Mulch Be?

The right depth depends on the situation. The general guideline is 2 to 3 inches for most residential garden beds and tree rings. Going deeper than 4 inches can actually harm your plants by suffocating roots and trapping excess moisture.

New beds with bare soil: Apply 3 inches. This provides full weed suppression coverage and establishes a solid base layer.

Existing beds with some remaining mulch: Top off to reach a total of 2-3 inches. If there's already an inch of old mulch, only add 1-2 inches of fresh material.

Around trees and shrubs: Keep it at 2-3 inches, and always leave a gap of 3-4 inches around the trunk or stem base. Never pile mulch against bark.

Pathways and play areas: You can go up to 4 inches for walkways and playground zones where the goal is ground coverage rather than plant health.

💡 Bags vs. Bulk: The Cost Difference

A standard bag of mulch from a hardware store contains about 2 cubic feet. You need roughly 13.5 bags to equal one cubic yard. At $4-6 per bag, that's $54-81 per yard — plus the time and vehicle space to haul them. Bulk delivery is significantly cheaper per yard and eliminates all the hauling work. For anything over 2-3 yards, bulk delivery almost always makes more sense.

Yards vs. Tons vs. Loads: Understanding Bulk Mulch Units

When you order bulk mulch, you'll see three common units. Here's what each one means:

Cubic Yards — the standard measurement for mulch. One cubic yard is a 3ft × 3ft × 3ft cube. This is what most residential customers order by.

Tons — a weight-based measurement used more often for heavier materials like gravel and stone, but some mulch suppliers use it too. One cubic yard of mulch typically weighs between 400-800 pounds depending on moisture content and material type. Wet, freshly-ground mulch weighs more than dry, aged material.

Loads — refers to a full truck load. The capacity depends on the truck size. A standard dump truck holds about 10-15 cubic yards. A semi-trailer can carry 40-90 yards. For large commercial projects, ordering by the load is often the most cost-effective approach.

Common Mulch Ordering Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Measuring in Feet Instead of Inches for Depth

This is the most common calculation error. If you enter "3" as depth thinking it means inches but the formula treats it as feet, you'll calculate 12 times more mulch than you actually need. Always double-check: depth should be in inches (2-3 for most jobs), then divide by 12 to convert to feet before multiplying.

Mistake #2: Forgetting to Account for Existing Mulch

If your beds still have an inch of mulch from last year, you don't need a full 3-inch application. Measure what's there and subtract. Over-mulching year after year leads to waterlogged beds and suffocated roots.

Mistake #3: Not Adding a Buffer

Beds are rarely perfect rectangles, ground is rarely perfectly level, and some mulch always gets scattered during spreading. Add 10% to your calculated total to make sure you have enough to finish the job without a second order.

Mistake #4: Buying Bags When You Need Yards

If your project needs more than about 2-3 cubic yards, the bagged route at a hardware store quickly becomes expensive and extremely labor-intensive. You'd need 27-40+ bags for just 3 yards. Bulk delivery drops it all on your driveway in minutes, at a fraction of the per-yard cost.

How to Prepare for Your Mulch Delivery

Before your bulk mulch arrives, a little preparation goes a long way:

Choose a drop spot. Most deliveries are dumped onto the driveway. Make sure the area is clear of vehicles and obstacles. If you want it placed somewhere specific (side yard, backyard gate, etc.), note that on your order.

Weed your beds first. Spreading mulch over existing weeds just buries the problem temporarily. Pull weeds and edge your beds before the mulch arrives.

Have tools ready. A wheelbarrow, garden rake, and flat shovel are all you need to spread bulk mulch. Most homeowners can spread 1-2 cubic yards in about an hour.

Schedule ahead. G&D Materials requires 28-48 hours advance notice for all deliveries. Plan your order a few days before you want to start spreading.

Frequently Asked Questions About Mulch Quantities

How many bags of mulch equal one cubic yard?

Approximately 13-14 bags (at the standard 2 cubic feet per bag). This is why bulk delivery is so much more practical for projects needing more than a couple of yards.

How many square feet does a yard of mulch cover?

At 2 inches deep: 162 sq ft. At 3 inches deep: 108 sq ft. At 4 inches deep: 81 sq ft.

How much does a cubic yard of mulch weigh?

Between 400 and 800 pounds, depending on the material type and moisture content. Dry hardwood mulch is on the lighter end. Freshly-ground or wet mulch weighs more.

How much mulch fits in a pickup truck?

A full-size pickup truck bed holds about 2-3 cubic yards, depending on the bed size and how high you load it. That's 3-4 trips for a moderate residential job — which is exactly why most people prefer bulk delivery.
